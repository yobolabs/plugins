# Pack: Conversion Craft

The lens for craft that converts - thumb-stop mechanics, glance-speed message clarity, CTA discipline, and direct-response fundamentals.

## 1. Scope

This lens owns the asset's persuasion mechanics: what interrupts the scroll, whether the message lands in one glance, whether the action is present, findable, and low-friction, and whether the ad keeps the promise trail intact to the landing experience.

Not covered here: the strategy itself - offer, audience, hooks, and final copy belong to ads-strategist and arrive via the brief; this lens shapes how the chosen message is DELIVERED visually. Also not here: reading order mechanics (visual-hierarchy), type craft (typography), palette (color-contrast), placement fitness (channel-grammar). Post-launch truth arrives through the feedback loop (Looker), which grades this lens's predictions.

## 2. Core craft principles

- **The scroll is the default outcome.** Nobody arrives wanting the ad. The first job is to interrupt a practiced, fast scrolling motion - honestly. Thumb-stop comes from two forces together: PATTERN BREAK (the asset contrasts with the ambient feed in value, color, subject, or simplicity) and INSTANT RELEVANCE (the viewer recognizes in a beat that this concerns them - their food, their neighborhood, their problem). Pattern break without relevance is clickbait; relevance without pattern break is invisible.
- **One ad, one message, one action.** Every additional message divides attention until none is received. The single-message discipline is the hardest sell to stakeholders and the most reliable craft rule in direct response. Variants exist so that other messages get their own asset.
- **Glance-speed comprehension is the bar.** In one glance the viewer should be able to answer: what is this, is it for me, what do I do. If comprehension requires reading in sequence, the asset is a document, not an ad.
- **Show the outcome, do not assert it.** A benefit demonstrated (the dish steaming, the room transformed, the queue gone) outperforms a benefit claimed in text. The image carries the proof; the words carry the offer.
- **Specificity converts.** A concrete particular (a nameable dish, a real interior, an actual before-state) beats an abstraction every time - it is simultaneously a conversion lever and an authenticity lever (authenticity pack AU-3). The interchangeability test: if a competitor could run the same asset unchanged, it is not specific enough.
- **The CTA is small but sovereign.** Presence is non-negotiable in direct response. It is verb-led, short, and the highest-contrast SMALL element in the frame, sitting at the eye path's exit (visual-hierarchy VH-6). It does not compete with the hero - it concludes it.
- **Keep the scent trail.** The ad is a promise about what the next tap delivers. Visual continuity between ad and landing surface (same offer, same tone, same imagery family) preserves trust; a bait-and-switch in look or offer burns the click and trains the audience to distrust the brand's ads.

## 3. Directive inventory

**CV-1 - Engineer the thumb-stop honestly**
- principle: pattern break + instant relevance, together.
- preconditions: feed and story placements (cards and heroes are sought out, not scrolled past - relevance still applies, pattern break less). The break is designed against the ambient feed recon describes, not against a generic feed.
- failure modes: shock or trick imagery that stops thumbs and burns trust (and trips authenticity's fake-detector); tasteful invisibility - an asset so feed-matched it is never seen (hier-no-focal); relevance signals so generic they signal no one (cliche-stock-energy).
- prompt hooks: "striking [subject] against a simple background", "unexpected close-up of [the product in use]", "bold graphic simplicity", plus the mission's relevance cue - "[the audience's recognizable setting/dish/moment]".

**CV-2 - One message per asset**
- principle: one ad, one message, one action - additional messages get their own variants.
- preconditions: always. The craft file enforces this at the per-asset level: each asset section names its single message; a second message in the same section is a design error, not richness.
- failure modes: offer + brand story + product range in one frame (hier-everything-shouts at message level); two CTAs splitting the action; carousel thinking on a single card.
- prompt hooks: process directive; its trace in prompts is simplicity - "single product", "one clear subject", "minimal composition".

**CV-3 - Pass the glance test**
- principle: glance-speed comprehension - what is this / is it for me / what do I do, answerable in one glance.
- preconditions: every asset; the test is run at render size on a phone (typography TY-1 supplies the proof condition). This is an expected-vs-broken line the crit pass and execute's L2 both check.
- failure modes: comprehension gated on reading body text (hier-text-wall); a clever visual that needs the caption to decode; category ambiguity (is this food or furniture?) at thumbnail size.
- prompt hooks: "immediately recognizable [product/category]", "clear unambiguous subject", "product shown in obvious use".

**CV-4 - CTA present, findable, and last**
- principle: the CTA is small but sovereign - present in every direct-response asset, verb-led, highest-contrast small element, at the exit of the eye path.
- preconditions: all direct-response missions. Pure awareness assets may resolve on the brand instead - that is a deliberate care-level/strategy call recorded in the brief, never a default. Platform-furnished CTA buttons (the placement's own button chrome) count; then the asset's job is to not fight them.
- failure modes: CTA absent because "the platform adds one" when it does not (hier-buried-cta); CTA competing with the hero for first read; CTA wording as noun-mush instead of a verb.
- prompt hooks: layout-stage directive; in generation, "clear space in the lower third for a button overlay" reserves the slot (accent color discipline from color-contrast CC-1).

**CV-5 - Demonstrate, don't decorate**
- principle: show the outcome - the image proves the benefit; the words state the offer.
- preconditions: any offer with a visible outcome (food, spaces, appearance, convenience). For invisible benefits (savings, speed), show the human moment of the benefit received, not a metaphor collage.
- failure modes: mood decoration with no product evidence (cliche-lifestyle-nobody-lives); outcome exaggerated past what the offer delivers - a claims problem, not just a taste problem (brand-implied-claim); stock gesture standing in for real use (cliche-stock-energy).
- prompt hooks: "[product] in actual use by [audience-plausible person]", "the finished [dish/room/result] as the hero", "authentic moment of [benefit happening]".

**CV-6 - Be specific or be skipped**
- principle: specificity converts - concrete particulars beat abstractions, and survive the interchangeability test.
- preconditions: always; the particulars come from recon (the merchant's actual product, place, and audience texture). When legal/claims M-rules limit what can be named, specificity moves to the imagery.
- failure modes: generic praise-scapes any competitor could run (cliche-generic-localism); abstract benefit language with abstract imagery squared; specificity faked with invented details that recon cannot back (a fake-detector trip and possibly a claims violation).
- prompt hooks: name the particulars - "[the specific dish]", "[the actual storefront type/neighborhood texture recon verified]", "[the real product variant]" - never "delicious food" or "beautiful home".

**CV-7 - Match the landing scent**
- principle: keep the scent trail - the ad's look and promise continue on the next surface.
- preconditions: any mission where the destination is known at design time (it should be - entity resolution pins it). The craft file records the landing reference per asset so the check is mechanical.
- failure modes: ad palette/imagery family absent from the landing page; offer wording differing between ad and destination; a premium-graded ad delivering to a bare-bones page (trust whiplash).
- prompt hooks: process directive - reuse the same palette hooks and subject family across ad and landing hero prompts.

## 4. Signature questions

1. In the recon-described feed this will actually run in, what makes this asset the pattern break - and what makes it instantly THIS audience's?
2. State the asset's one message in one sentence. Now find anything in the frame not serving it.
3. One glance at render size: what is it, who is it for, what do you do? Did any answer need a second glance?
4. Where is the action, is it a verb, and is it the highest-contrast small element at the eye's exit?
5. What does the IMAGE prove about the benefit that the text merely claims?
6. Could a competitor ship this asset unchanged? If yes, what particular from recon would make it unmistakably ours?
7. Tap through in your head: does the landing surface keep the ad's promise in look and offer?
8. What does the feedback hook predict for this asset (expected winner? expected reaction?) - and is that prediction written down for the Looker grade?

## 5. Placement / asset-class mapping

| Placement class | Weight | What this lens demands here |
|---|---|---|
| Feed image (square / tall portrait) | primary | Full thumb-stop mechanics; single message; CTA or platform button acknowledged; glance test at feed render size |
| Story / full-screen vertical | primary | Relevance must land even faster (surface is tap-through); action in the lower third above the platform rail; casual dialect carries the demonstration |
| Marketplace / product card | primary | Conversion here is comparison-winning: true product, legible price, zero ambiguity; the grid IS the glance test |
| Banner / leaderboard (wide) | secondary | One message and the action, nothing else fits; specificity carried by wording and mark |
| Hero / landing image | secondary | The scent-trail anchor: this is the surface the ads must match; demonstration can run deeper here |

## 6. Exemplar pointers

Slots for the exemplar stories to fill; do not invent files:

- exemplar needed: a thumb-stop pair - the same offer, one pattern-break-with-relevance version and one feed-invisible version, with the mechanic annotated (anchor for CV-1)
- exemplar needed: a single-message asset beside its everything-in-one predecessor (anchor for CV-2/CV-3)
- exemplar needed: a demonstrated-benefit asset vs an asserted-benefit asset for the same product (anchor for CV-5)
