# GTM — Brief

Distilled durable frames. Load before sparring any positioning, wedge, motion, or pricing plan. Complements the `business-strategy` pack (Power, five forces, base-rate escape) — load both for GTM spars.

## Positioning is an input decision, not messaging (Dunford)
Five components set in order: competitive alternatives (what customers would REALLY do without you, including spreadsheets and interns) → unique attributes → value (with proof) → target customers who care most → market category that frames it all; trends only as garnish. Most weak positioning fails at step one — founders position against named competitors instead of the customer's actual alternative.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Dunford, Obviously Awesome, 2019 · grade: B

## Category choice: existing / subcategory / create
Three options with wildly different costs: position in an existing category (cheapest — buyers arrive with expectations and budget), carve a subcategory (moderate), create a category (most expensive — you pay to educate the market AND to be believed). Category creation is a funded-incumbent's game; default to an existing frame unless the product genuinely breaks it.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Dunford, Obviously Awesome, 2019 (category-selection guidance); see base-rates.md category-creation caution entry · grade: B

## Beachhead / wedge (Moore)
Pragmatist buyers demand references from their own segment, so a startup must dominate one narrow segment before general expansion — whole product for one niche beats partial product for many. The chasm is between visionaries (buy vision) and pragmatists (buy references). Wedge test: one user, one job, clearly beats the incumbent alternative.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Moore, Crossing the Chasm, 1991 (3rd ed. 2014) · grade: A (canonical, decades of practitioner validation)

## Bowling-pin sequencing
Expansion order is chosen by reference-and-capability adjacency: the next segment must share references or whole-product components with the last. A segment list without an adjacency argument is a wish list, not a sequence.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Moore, Inside the Tornado, 1995 · grade: B

## Jobs to be done
Customers hire products for a job; the competition is anything else hireable — including non-consumption and "we just don't fix this". Ask what gets FIRED when you're hired, and what the firing costs (switching, politics, ego).
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Christensen et al., "Know Your Customers' Jobs to Be Done," HBR 2016 · grade: A

## Motion follows ACV (Janz's five ways)
$100M ARR = 10M "flies" × $10 · 1M "mice" × $100 · 100k "rabbits" × $1k · 10k "deer" × $10k · 1k "elephants" × $100k. Each tier dictates its motion: flies/mice = self-serve/viral, rabbits = low-touch inbound + product-led, deer = inside sales, elephants = field sales. The killing mismatch: a rabbit-priced product with a deer-cost motion (or SMB ACV with enterprise support load). Price and motion must be chosen together.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Janz, "Five ways to build a $100M business," Point Nine / The Angel VC blog, 2014 · grade: B

## PLG vs sales-led is a buyer-and-time-to-value question
PLG works when the end user can experience the value alone, fast, without procurement (Slack, Figma). It fails when value needs org-wide adoption, integration work, or the payer never touches the product — then user-love produces adoption without revenue (see failure-modes: Docker, RethinkDB). Bottom-up products need TWO motions: an adoption motion aimed at users and a monetization motion aimed at the budget owner.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Bush, Product-Led Growth, 2019; OpenView PLG research corpus (openview archive; OpenView wound down 2023 — successor practitioner source is Poyar's Growth Unhinged) · grade: B

## Founder-led sales before sales hires
The first ~10–20 sales belong to the founders — not for revenue but to learn the objections, the real buyer, and whether a repeatable sale exists. Hiring a VP Sales to "figure out" an unproven motion is buying an expensive experiment run by someone with less context. Scale sales only after the sale is repeatable by someone who isn't a founder.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Roberge, The Sales Acceleration Formula, 2015; Graham, "Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013 · grade: B

## Value-metric pricing
Price on the metric that scales with the value the customer receives (seats, usage, revenue processed) — the wrong metric caps expansion revenue and misprices heavy users. Per-seat when value tracks people, usage-based when value tracks consumption; hybrid is common. Pricing model is a near-one-way door: early customers anchor on it, and migrations churn them.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Price Intelligently/Paddle pricing research corpus; Poyar (OpenView/Growth Unhinged) usage-based pricing playbooks, 2021–2023 · grade: B

## Buyers mostly buy without you in the room
B2B buyers spend ~17% of the buying journey meeting suppliers — 5–6% with any ONE vendor's reps — and a majority now prefer a rep-free experience. Implication: the content, docs, pricing page, and peer references ARE the sales motion for most of the journey; a GTM plan that only works when a rep is talking is fighting the base rate.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Gartner B2B buying-journey research (gartner.com/en/sales/insights/b2b-buying-journey); Gartner sales-survey press releases 2025-06-25 (61% prefer rep-free) and 2026-03-09 (67%) · grade: A
