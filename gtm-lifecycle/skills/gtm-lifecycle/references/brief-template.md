# strategy-brief.md — GTM brief schema

Nine sections, in order. Dense prose + tables; no filler. Header carries status, topic, decision owner, and what happens next.

```markdown
# {Product} GTM — Strategy Brief

**Status:** DRAFT for GATE 1 (brainstorm {date}) · gtm-lifecycle Phase 1
**Topic:** `{project}/gtm` · **Decision owner:** {human}
**Next:** GATE 1 approve → spar the load-bearing bets → war-game the one-way doors → synthesize

---

## 1. Objective
What this GTM push is FOR (Q1 verbatim intent, sharpened): the decision to be made,
the horizon, what success looks like. Name what it is NOT (e.g. "this is a positioning
decision, not a scaling push — scaling comes after the wedge is proven").

## 2. Where we are (honest baseline)
The Q2 signal, stated without spin: usage/traction as it actually is ("internal dogfood
only" is a legitimate baseline — say it), proof assets that exist (real apps, references,
case-study material), and what kind of motion that baseline implies (e.g. dogfood-to-product).

## 3. Positioning hypothesis (to be tested, not assumed)
- **Wedge candidate:** Q3 answer + why — flag it if it's conviction rather than evidence
  ("the founder's conviction; widest-TAM / hardest-to-differentiate choice — a primary spar target").
- **Differentiator:** Q4 answer as a mechanism/mental-model, with the crowded field named.
- **One-liner (draft):** a single positioning sentence. Marked draft.

## 4. THE central unresolved question — who pays?
Only if Q6 stayed open (the buyer-crux rule). Candidate motions as a table:

| # | Motion | Buyer | End user | The product is… |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | … | … | … | … |
| B | … | … | … | … |
| C | … | … | … | … |

State explicitly: the brief does NOT pick one; each is a competing hypothesis for spar
to attack and war-game to fight; the winner is whichever survives the constraints with
a credible path to revenue. (If Q6 WAS answered confidently, this section becomes
"4. The buyer + motion" — one paragraph, and the choice itself becomes a bet in §5.)

## 5. Load-bearing bets (spar attacks each of these)
Named, numbered, FALSIFIABLE — an observation could kill each one. "If one dies, the
strategy above it changes." 3–7 bets. Naming convention `B-<Name>: <claim>`. Typical
GTM bets: B-Differentiator (buyers pay for it, field can't copy it fast),
B-Generalize (the dogfood job exists externally), B-ProductLed (motion works without
a sales force), B-Reachable (a buyer is reachable inside runway), B-Moat, B-DualTrack
(the team survives running two tracks).

## 6. Constraints (fixed — these rule motions in/out)
Q7 answers as RULES, each with its consequence ("no sales force → whatever wins must be
product-led"). Then the **implication/squeeze** paragraph: the tension the constraints
create across the candidate motions, and any honest extra option the squeeze surfaces —
captured for spar to test against the named motions.

## 7. Channels · pricing · sequencing (rough — firmed after spar)
Marked rough. Channels that fit the constraints; pricing flagged unresolved/motion-
dependent if it is (and one-way-ish → §8); a candidate sequencing with its own
sequence-is-a-bet flag.

## 8. One-way doors (candidates for war-game)
Moves that are expensive/impossible to reverse, one line each on WHY irreversible:
public positioning commit · pricing-model commit · architecture/tenancy lock-ins ·
starving an existing revenue line · exclusive channel/partner deals.

## 9. Open questions (for GATE 1 / to hand to recon)
What the human must supply (runway months, the "revenue soon" number, team shape) and
what recon should hunt (named competitors on the differentiator axis, uncounted inbound,
whether a committed second bet is real or hypothetical).
```

Gold-standard instance: the Northwind brief this template was distilled from — objective as reposition-not-scale, dogfood-only baseline stated plainly, three competing motions left open, seven falsifiable bets, four constraints squeezing all three motions into an honest fourth reading.
