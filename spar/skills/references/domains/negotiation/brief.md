# Negotiation — Brief

Distilled durable frames. Every spar over a deal, offer, or dispute loads these first.

## BATNA / reservation price / ZOPA
Power in negotiation = quality of your best alternative to agreement (BATNA). Reservation price = the worst deal you'll accept, derived from BATNA, written down BEFORE talks. ZOPA = overlap between the parties' reservation prices; if none exists, the goal is to change alternatives, not to persuade harder.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Fisher & Ury, Getting to Yes, 1981 · grade: A

## Interests vs positions
Positions are stated demands; interests are the needs underneath. Incompatible positions often hide compatible interests (the orange: one wants juice, one wants peel). Diagnose interests by asking why, and why-not on their refusals.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Fisher & Ury, Getting to Yes, 1981 · grade: A

## First offers anchor — hard
First offers correlate r ≈ .50 with final outcomes across the meta-analytic record; the first-mover advantage survives expertise, though experience attenuates it. Make the first offer when you know the range; when facing an anchor, re-anchor explicitly rather than counter incrementally from their number.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Orr & Guthrie, Ohio State J. on Dispute Resolution 2005 (meta-analysis, r=.497); Galinsky & Mussweiler, JPSP 2001 · grade: A

## Precision signals knowledge — until it doesn't
Precise offers ($4,985 vs $5,000) draw smaller counteroffers from amateurs (precision implies information). Against experts, over-precision backfires — they read unjustified precision as bluff.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Mason, Lee, Wiley & Ames, JESP 2013; Loschelder et al., Psych Science 2016 · grade: A

## Distributive vs integrative — expand before dividing
Single-issue negotiation is pure claiming. Add issues, logroll (trade what's cheap-for-you/valuable-to-them), use MESOs (multiple equivalent simultaneous offers) to reveal preferences without conceding. ~20% of negotiators reach lose-lose deals on objectively compatible issues because neither asked.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Thompson & Hrebec, Psych Bulletin 1996 (32 studies); Medvec & Galinsky on MESO, Negotiation (Kellogg practice) · grade: A

## Agreement is not the goal — value is
Impasse aversion drives negotiators to accept deals worse than their own BATNA ("deal fever"). The test of a negotiation is value against alternatives, not whether ink hit paper.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Tuncel, Mislin, Kesebir & Pinkley, "Agreement Attraction and Impasse Aversion," Psych Science 2016 · grade: A

## Negotiate process before substance
Agenda, participants, timeline, decision authority, and what "final" means are themselves negotiable and shape the outcome more than mid-game tactics. Verify the person across the table can actually say yes.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Malhotra & Bazerman, Negotiation Genius, 2007 · grade: B

## The relationship is part of the payoff
Subjective value (feeling respected, process fairness) predicts future deals, compliance, and renegotiation behavior — measurably beyond economic terms. First minutes set it: conversational dynamics in the first 5 minutes predicted ~30% of outcome variance.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Curhan, Elfenbein & Xu (Subjective Value Inventory), JPSP 2006; Curhan & Pentland, JAP 2007 · grade: A

## Incentives of agents ≠ incentives of principals
Anyone negotiating on your behalf optimizes their own payoff curve. Real-estate agents selling their OWN homes get +3.7% price and wait 9.5 days longer than when selling clients' homes.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Levitt & Syverson, Review of Economics & Statistics 2008 · grade: A
