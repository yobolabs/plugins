# Negotiation — Failure Modes

Format: signal → cause → countermove. Red-team plays these against any deal plan.

## Anchoring capitulation
- signal: all subsequent numbers are adjustments of the counterparty's opening; your side never states its own independently derived figure.
- cause: insufficient adjustment from any salient anchor; preparation gap — no reservation price written before contact.
- countermove: set reservation price + target ex ante; if anchored, name the tactic, re-anchor with a justified number from a different basis (comps, cost build-up), don't split-the-difference from their frame.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Galinsky & Mussweiler, JPSP 2001; Orr & Guthrie 2005 meta (r=.497 first offer → outcome) · grade: A

## Exploding-offer pressure
- signal: artificial deadline attached to a take-it-or-leave-it package; pressure peaks exactly when your alternatives can't be mobilized in time.
- cause: counterparty converting time asymmetry into leverage; your side treating the stated deadline as physics.
- countermove: test it — the Farpoint Gambit: accept conditionally on the expiring element ("I accept, provided X"), forcing them to either negotiate or expose the deadline as bluff. Ask what specifically changes at the deadline and who set it.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Robinson, "Defusing the Exploding Offer: The Farpoint Gambit," Negotiation Journal 1995 · grade: A

## Deal fever (agreement bias)
- signal: team talks about "getting it closed" more than about the terms; deal terms drift below the pre-set reservation price; impasse discussed as failure.
- cause: impasse aversion — agreement itself becomes the goal; escalation after long investment in the process (sunk negotiation cost).
- countermove: written BATNA + reservation price before talks, held by someone NOT at the table with authority to enforce; celebrate walk-aways that beat bad deals.
- case: RBS-led consortium's €71B ABN AMRO acquisition (2007), pursued as targets deteriorated; RBS required a £45.5B UK bailout in 2008; the FSA's 2011 report found due diligence amounted to "two lever-arch folders and a CD."
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Tuncel et al., Psych Science 2016; FSA Board Report on RBS failure, Dec 2011 · grade: A

## Fixed-pie assumption
- signal: every issue treated as zero-sum; no questions asked about the other side's priorities; single-issue haggling over price.
- cause: default competitive frame ("mythical fixed pie"); preparation focused only on own positions.
- countermove: rank issues by priority for both sides pre-game; trade across differently-valued issues (logroll); present MESOs to elicit preferences; check for fully compatible issues explicitly.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Bazerman & Neale, Negotiating Rationally, 1992; Thompson & Hrebec, Psych Bulletin 1996 (~20% lose-lose on compatible issues) · grade: A

## Winner's curse in competitive bidding
- signal: you won easily / were the high bidder by a wide margin; valuation relied on the same public information as everyone else's.
- cause: with common-value assets, the winner is the bidder with the largest overestimate; failing to condition your estimate on the fact of winning.
- countermove: shade bids by expected overestimate; ask "what does winning tell me about what the losers knew?"; cap via pre-committed walk-away set outside the auction room.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Bazerman & Samuelson, "I Won the Auction But Don't Want the Prize," J. Conflict Resolution 1983 · grade: A

## Escalation spiral (dollar auction)
- signal: both sides now justify spend by what's already committed; "losing" has become identity, not economics; costs exceed the prize's value.
- cause: competitive arousal + sunk costs + audience; bidding structures where second place pays too.
- countermove: pre-set maximum commitment before entering; assign a non-involved kill authority; reframe from "beat them" to "net value at exit."
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Shubik, "The Dollar Auction Game," J. Conflict Resolution 1971; Malhotra, Ku & Murnighan, "When Winning Is Everything," HBR 2008 · grade: A

## Reactive devaluation
- signal: an identical proposal is rated worse because the counterparty offered it ("if they want it, it must be bad for us").
- cause: attribution of hostile intent to the source; concession read as signal of hidden value.
- countermove: blind evaluation — assess terms against your interests scorecard before attribution; route proposals through neutral framing (mediator text).
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Ross & Stillinger, "Barriers to Conflict Resolution," Negotiation Journal 1991 (Stanford arms-reduction experiments) · grade: A

## Concealing your deadline
- signal: your side hides its time constraint, then makes accelerating unilateral concessions as the date approaches.
- cause: intuition says revealing weakness; in fact concealment means the counterparty doesn't compress their own concessions to your window.
- countermove: reveal final deadlines — experimentally, revealing improves the revealer's outcomes by forcing the counterparty to negotiate within the real window.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Moore, "The Unexpected Benefits of Final Deadlines in Negotiation," JESP 2004 · grade: A

## Negotiating with the wrong person
- signal: every concession you extract gets "taken back to the committee"; their side's agreement never binds; your concessions are pocketed and re-traded.
- cause: authority asymmetry — you have mandate, they have a ratification layer (real or manufactured).
- countermove: negotiate process first: confirm decision authority and ratification path before substance; mirror their structure (add your own ratifier) if they won't collapse theirs.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Malhotra & Bazerman, Negotiation Genius, 2007; Schelling, The Strategy of Conflict, 1960 (commitment via limited authority) · grade: B

## Agent incentive misalignment
- signal: your agent (broker, banker, lawyer) pushes speed-to-close over price; advice conveniently tracks their fee structure.
- cause: agents capture a sliver of marginal gains but 100% of deal-completion fees — effort beyond "good enough to close" is privately unprofitable for them.
- countermove: restructure fees toward the margin you care about (thresholds, ratchets); get a no-deal-compensated second opinion; benchmark against agent-own-account behavior.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Levitt & Syverson, RES 2008 (agents' own homes: +3.7% price, +9.5 days on market) · grade: A
