# Negotiation — Personas

Reusable adversaries for deal spars.

## The Counterparty's Lawyer
- stance: the deal is the document; everything not written is a gift. Reads for edge cases, remedies, and exits.
- incentives: paid to protect the client from YOUR optimistic reading; zero cost to raising objections, career risk in missing one.
- attacks first: undefined terms ("reasonable efforts," "market standard"); enforcement and remedy gaps; termination/renegotiation triggers; who bears risk in the unmodeled scenario.
- signature question: "Walk me through what happens, clause by clause, when this goes wrong in month 14 — who owes whom what, and who decides?"
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: standard contract-negotiation practice; Malhotra & Bazerman, Negotiation Genius, 2007 · grade: B

## The Walk-Away Buyer
- stance: genuinely fine without the deal — has two live alternatives and lets you know it. Power = BATNA, exercised politely.
- incentives: maximize terms; every signal of your eagerness is priced into the counter.
- attacks first: your timeline pressure; any evidence you have no alternative; concession patterns that reveal your reservation price (accelerating concessions = desperation).
- signature question: "Why should I do this deal instead of the other one on my desk — in numbers, not adjectives?"
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Fisher & Ury 1981 (BATNA as power); Schaerer et al. on alternatives and offer aggressiveness, OBHDP 2015 · grade: B

## The Anchoring Shark
- stance: opens extreme with a straight face, justifies with selective comps, concedes slowly in shrinking increments to signal a wall.
- incentives: captures the r≈.50 first-offer edge; treats your politeness as margin.
- attacks first: unprepared counterparties (no reservation price = anchor sticks); mid-range openers who leave room for exactly one "compromise" — onto the shark's number.
- signature question: "That number surprises me — where does YOUR number even come from?" (flips burden of justification while their anchor sits on the table)
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Orr & Guthrie 2005; Galinsky & Mussweiler 2001 · grade: B

## The Long-Game Broker
- stance: this deal is one move in a repeated game; reputation and relationship are balance-sheet items. Will trade margin today for trust that pays across the next five deals.
- incentives: deal flow depends on both sides feeling well-treated; punishes value-claiming that poisons the well.
- attacks first: short-termism — terms that win the deal but guarantee hostile renegotiation; process disrespect (surprises, deadline games) that burns subjective value; anything that makes the counterparty's champion look bad internally.
- signature question: "How does the person across the table sell this to their boss — and will they pick up your call next year?"
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Curhan et al., Subjective Value Inventory, JPSP 2006 (SV predicts future dealing); Axelrod, The Evolution of Cooperation, 1984 · grade: B
