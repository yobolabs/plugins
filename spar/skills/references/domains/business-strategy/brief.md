# Business Strategy — Brief

Distilled durable frames. Load before sparring any market-entry, product, or competitive plan.

## Strategy = trade-offs, not goals
A strategy states where you will NOT compete and what you deliberately forgo; a list of ambitions ("be the leading X") is not one. Rumelt's kernel: diagnosis → guiding policy → coherent action. Most "strategies" fail the kernel test.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Porter, "What Is Strategy?", HBR 1996; Rumelt, Good Strategy Bad Strategy, 2011 · grade: A

## Power: what stops copying
Returns persist only with a Power — 7 canonical: scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power. A plan that works without a Power gets competed away exactly when it starts working.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Helmer, 7 Powers, 2016 · grade: B

## Counter-positioning predicts incumbent response
Incumbents fail to respond when responding would cannibalize their existing economics — not because they're blind. Model the incumbent's P&L to predict their reaction: they'll ignore what's unprofitable to copy and crush what isn't.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Helmer, 7 Powers, 2016; Christensen, The Innovator's Dilemma, 1997 · grade: B

## Jobs to be done
Customers hire products for a job; competition is anything else hireable for it, including non-consumption. Segment by job, not demographics — the milkshake competes with bananas and boredom, not other milkshakes.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Christensen et al., "Know Your Customers' Jobs to Be Done," HBR 2016 · grade: A

## Distribution > product
"Poor sales rather than bad product is the most common cause of failure." Channel economics (CAC vs LTV at maturity, channel saturation) decide viability more often than product quality; a product that can't be economically distributed is a hobby.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Thiel, Zero to One, 2014; corroborating: CB Insights startup post-mortems (product-market-fit + go-to-market causes dominate) · grade: B

## Five forces / profit pools
Industry structure sets the ceiling: supplier/buyer power, entry barriers, substitutes, rivalry. Map where the profit pool actually sits in the value chain before entering — revenue and profit routinely sit in different layers (airlines vs GDS; PCs vs Intel/Microsoft).
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Porter, HBR 1979/2008; Gadiesh & Gilbert, "Profit Pools," HBR 1998 · grade: A

## Why now
Every durable new entry has a "why now" — a technology, regulatory, or behavior shift that made the old equilibrium unstable. Without one, either it's been tried (find the bodies) or an incumbent does it first.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: practitioner canon — e.g., Gurley/Benchmark and a16z investment memos; codified in Sequoia pitch template ("Why now?") · grade: B

## Disruption is a specific mechanism, not a synonym for "new"
Low-end/new-market entrants win when incumbents rationally retreat upmarket; sustaining innovations favor incumbents. Most self-described disruption is sustaining innovation in a costume — expect the incumbent to win those.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Christensen, The Innovator's Dilemma, 1997; Lepore critique + Christensen response, 2014 (definition discipline) · grade: A

## Outside view on entry
Before any entry plan: pioneer failure ≈ 47%, M&A value destruction 70–90%, ~65% of VC deals lose money. The plan must explain which base rate it escapes and how — "our team is better" is not a mechanism.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: see base-rates.md (Golder & Tellis 1993; Christensen HBR 2011; Correlation Ventures) · grade: A
