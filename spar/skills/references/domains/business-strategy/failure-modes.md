# Business Strategy — Failure Modes

Format: signal → cause → countermove. Red-team's guaranteed reactions for strategy spars.

## Incumbent-response blindness
- signal: the plan's financial model holds competitor behavior constant; "we'll undercut them by 30%" with no move-two.
- cause: strategy built as a single-player optimization; no one owns the incumbent's playbook.
- countermove: war-game move two explicitly — price the incumbent's retaliation options (price match, bundle, exclusive contracts, litigation) against their P&L; enter where response is unprofitable for them (counter-positioning).
- case: People Express — 5th-largest US airline by 1985 on cheap fares; American's yield-management response (Ultimate Super Savers, 1985) matched prices on select seats without matching cost structure; People Express collapsed into a Texas Air takeover by 1986.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: HBS case material on People Express; Cross, Revenue Management, 1997 (Crandall's account); Helmer, 7 Powers, 2016 · grade: B

## Top-down TAM fallacy
- signal: market sized as "X% of a $NB market"; the deck's TAM slide cites an analyst aggregate, never a customer count.
- cause: percentages of big numbers feel rigorous while dodging the only question that matters — who buys, at what price, reached how.
- countermove: bottom-up only — (identifiable buyers) × (achievable price) × (achievable share of the channel); name the first 100 customers; reconcile against incumbent revenues in the niche.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: practitioner canon — Gurley, "How to Miss By a Mile" (Above the Crowd, 2014, on Uber TAM debate mechanics); standard VC diligence practice · grade: B

## Disruption denial (incumbent inertia)
- signal: the threat is dismissed as low-quality/niche ("our customers won't want that"); resource allocation still tracks the legacy margin structure while the entrant improves.
- cause: rational short-term economics — serving the disruptor's market dilutes margins; processes and values filter out the small market.
- countermove: autonomous unit with its own P&L chartered to attack the core; measure the entrant's improvement RATE, not current quality.
- case: Kodak built the first digital camera (Sasson, 1975), held digital patents, and still filed Chapter 11 in 2012 protecting film margins. Blockbuster declined to buy Netflix for $50M (2000); bankrupt 2010.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Christensen, The Innovator's Dilemma, 1997; NYT/WSJ Kodak bankruptcy coverage 2012; Randolph, That Will Never Work, 2019 (participant account) · grade: A

## Premature scaling
- signal: hiring/marketing/inventory scale ahead of retention and unit-economics proof; growth spend defended by projected, not observed, LTV.
- cause: confusing funding milestones with product-market fit; investor pressure to deploy.
- countermove: gate scale-up on cohort retention and contribution margin at current scale; scale the proven channel, not the org chart.
- case: Webvan — $800M+ raised, IPO'd 1999, built automated warehouses across 8+ metros before proving one; bankrupt July 2001.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Startup Genome Report 2011 (~70% of startups in sample scaled prematurely); documented Webvan financials (SEC filings, contemporaneous coverage) · grade: B

## Strategy-as-goals (no kernel)
- signal: the "strategy" is a target list (revenue, share, "world-class") plus values; no diagnosis of the problem, no explicit choice of what NOT to do.
- cause: goal-setting rituals substitute for the harder work of diagnosis; consensus processes sand off every sharp choice.
- countermove: force the kernel — diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent actions; require the "reject list" (what we will not do); test each action for consistency with the policy.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Rumelt, Good Strategy Bad Strategy, 2011; Porter, "What Is Strategy?", HBR 1996 · grade: A

## Adjacency creep / diworsification
- signal: acquisitions or line extensions justified by "synergy" with no shared customer, channel, or capability; core business flat while management attention scatters.
- cause: growth pressure exceeding the core's headroom; empire incentives; synergy models built to justify a decided deal.
- countermove: adjacency test — shares customers, channel, or cost structure with the core? price the deal against the 70–90% M&A value-destruction base rate; demand the integration plan before signing.
- case: Quaker Oats bought Snapple for $1.7B (1994), distribution model clashed with Gatorade's; sold for $300M (1997). DaimlerChrysler: $36B merger (1998) → Chrysler sold to Cerberus for $7.4B (2007).
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Christensen et al., "The New M&A Playbook," HBR 2011; documented deal financials (contemporaneous filings/coverage) · grade: A

## Build-it-and-they-will-come
- signal: roadmap dense, go-to-market slide thin ("virality," "word of mouth"); no named channel with modeled CAC.
- cause: product culture treats distribution as an afterthought; founders extrapolate early-adopter enthusiasm to the mainstream.
- countermove: one proven, priced channel before scale; treat CAC-at-maturity (post channel saturation) as the number, not launch CAC.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Thiel, Zero to One, 2014; CB Insights post-mortems (poor product-market fit 43%, and GTM causes, 2025 analysis of 431 shutdowns) · grade: B

## Metric gaming at strategy level (Goodhart)
- signal: the North Star metric climbs while customer outcomes/trust decay; frontline incentives reward the number, not the goal.
- cause: strategy operationalized as a single target with compensation attached; the proxy is cheaper to move than the reality.
- countermove: counter-metrics (quality, complaints, churn) paired with every growth target; audit the tails of the incentive distribution — that's where gaming shows first.
- case: Wells Fargo cross-selling ("eight is great") — ~3.5M unauthorized accounts opened; $3B DOJ/SEC settlement (2020); CEO resignation; sales-goal program abolished.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: DOJ settlement announcement, Feb 2020; House Financial Services Committee reports 2016–17 · grade: A

## Platform dependency
- signal: >50% of acquisition or revenue rides one platform's policy (app store, marketplace, social algorithm, single customer); "partnership" cited as a moat.
- cause: the platform's incentives drift as IT matures — it captures its complements' margin or changes rules for its own engagement goals.
- countermove: cap single-channel dependence; build direct relationships (email lists, contracts, multi-cloud); price a policy-change scenario into the plan.
- case: Zynga — built on Facebook virality; Facebook's 2011–12 feed/notification changes plus mobile shift cut Zynga's stock >75% within a year of IPO (bookings collapse documented in 2012 10-K).
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Zynga 2012 10-K risk factors + financials; contemporaneous coverage (WSJ/Reuters 2012) · grade: A

## Customer-hostile repositioning (skipping the test)
- signal: sweeping simultaneous change to pricing/brand/experience justified by internal conviction ("customers will learn to love it"); no A/B, no staged rollout.
- cause: leadership pattern-matching from a different customer base; treating a two-way door decision as one big one-way leap.
- countermove: stage the rollout; test on a region/cohort; define retreat triggers in advance.
- case: JC Penney under Ron Johnson (2012) — eliminated coupons/sales for "fair and square" pricing chain-wide, no testing; FY2012 revenue fell ~25% (~$4.3B); Johnson out in 17 months.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: JC Penney FY2012 results (10-K); HBR and press retrospectives 2013 · grade: A

## Constraint inflation (self-imposed)
- signal: a stated constraint quietly broadened in use — "no sales force" becomes "no selling"; "product-led" becomes "no outbound of any kind"; a motion dies citing the broad form.
- cause: constraints treated as identity, not as protections of a specific limit; nobody restates what the rule actually protects.
- countermove: restate every fixed constraint as the real limit it protects + the cheapest compliant version of the action it appears to forbid (founder-led selling to N≤5 design partners is not a sales force); re-test any constraint-eliminated motion against the narrowed form.
- case: CadraOS GTM calibration (2026-07) — framework inflated "no sales force" to "no selling", killed the active motion on it, and shipped a passive detector; the direct run challenged the constraint and derived a compliant founder-led motion.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: spar framework calibration, references/capability-gap.md · grade: B

## Passive detector where signal must be manufactured
- signal: the strategy gates on a pull signal (installs, inbound, badge clicks) after the run's own facts establish pull=0 and slow channels; expected time-to-verdict exceeds the runway or decision clock.
- cause: build-strong teams prefer instruments to conversations; passive detection feels rigorous while dodging the ask.
- countermove: manufacture the signal — direct ask, paid design partners, time-boxed outreach — designed to terminate unambiguously; the detector's expected time-to-verdict must beat the mission clock, or take the active alternative.
- case: CadraOS GTM calibration (2026-07) — 2-quarter gallery/tripwire whose most likely outcome was "test never ran — no verdict" inside a short-runway mission; a 7-day $0 outbound smoke test existed.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: spar framework calibration, references/capability-gap.md · grade: B
