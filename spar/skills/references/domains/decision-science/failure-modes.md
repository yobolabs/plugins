# Decision Science — Failure Modes

Format: signal → cause → countermove. Red-team uses these as guaranteed reactions.

## Sunk-cost escalation
- signal: justification for continuing cites what's already been spent ("we've come too far to stop"), not forward-looking returns.
- cause: loss framing of abandonment; personal responsibility for the original decision amplifies it (Staw 1976: responsible decision-makers doubled down hardest).
- countermove: new-owner test — "would a fresh CEO start this today?" (Intel's 1985 memory exit: Grove & Moore asked exactly this, killed the founding business). Set kill criteria ex ante.
- case: Concorde — est. £70M dev cost (1962), final ~£1.1–1.3B; both governments knew it was uneconomic years before launch and continued ("Concorde fallacy").
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Staw, OBHP 1976; Arkes & Blumer, "The Psychology of Sunk Cost," OBHDP 1985; Grove, Only the Paranoid Survive, 1996 · grade: A

## Inside-view planning (planning fallacy)
- signal: single-point schedule/budget built bottom-up from task lists; no reference to how similar efforts actually fared.
- cause: plans model the best case; the distribution of surprises is invisible from inside the plan.
- countermove: reference-class forecast first, adjust second; apply distribution-based uplift, not a % buffer.
- case: students estimated thesis completion at 33.9 days; actual 55.5 days; <30% finished by their own estimate (Buehler et al. 1994). Sydney Opera House: AU$7M estimate (1957) → AU$102M actual (1973).
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Buehler, Griffin & Ross, JPSP 1994; Flyvbjerg et al., "Underestimating Costs in Public Works Projects," JAPA 2002 · grade: A

## Resulting (outcome bias)
- signal: decision review starts from how it turned out; winners' processes canonized, losers' processes purged.
- cause: outcome is visible, process is not; hindsight makes the observed outcome look inevitable.
- countermove: grade decisions against information available at the time; keep a decision journal with probabilities recorded at decision time.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Baron & Hershey, JPSP 1988; Duke, Thinking in Bets, 2018 · grade: A

## Confirmation-seeking tests
- signal: the pilot/experiment has no outcome that would count as failure; success criteria drafted after data arrives.
- cause: hypothesis testing defaults to seeking confirming instances (Wason 2-4-6: subjects almost never test rule-breaking triples).
- countermove: pre-register the falsifier — "what result kills this?" — before running anything; run the disconfirming test first.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Wason, QJEP 1960 · grade: A

## Groupthink / premature consensus
- signal: unanimity arrives fast; dissenters self-censor; "everyone was in the room and agreed."
- cause: cohesion + directive leadership + insulation from outside estimates; discussion before independent judgment correlates the errors.
- countermove: written independent estimates before discussion; assigned red team; leader speaks last.
- case: Bay of Pigs 1961 — Kennedy's advisors privately doubted, publicly concurred; Janis built the groupthink model on it. Kennedy then restructured process for the Cuban Missile Crisis (subgroups, devil's advocates).
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Janis, Victims of Groupthink, 1972 · grade: A

## Irreversibility blindness
- signal: a one-way door (hire of an executive, public commitment, platform migration, lease signature) decided with two-way-door casualness — or the reverse: trivial reversible calls escalated to committees.
- cause: no explicit reversibility classification step in the decision process.
- countermove: classify door type first; for one-way doors buy optionality (staging, pilots, exit clauses) even at a premium.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Bezos, Amazon 2015 shareholder letter · grade: A

## Ruin-blind EV maximization
- signal: "the expected value is positive" used to justify a bet whose downside is fatal; leverage stacked on high-confidence models.
- cause: EV math assumes you survive to play again; correlated tail risk ignored because the model says it's near-impossible.
- countermove: cap position size below ruin threshold (Kelly or fractional Kelly); ask "can we take this bet 100 times?"
- case: LTCM 1998 — Nobel-grade models, ~25:1 leverage; lost $4.6B in under four months when correlations spiked; NY Fed organized a $3.6B bailout.
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Lowenstein, When Genius Failed, 2000; Federal Reserve testimony (Greenspan, Oct 1998) · grade: A

## Anchored estimation
- signal: estimates cluster around the first number spoken — even when that number is arbitrary or came from the counterparty.
- cause: anchoring adjusts insufficiently from any salient starting value; expertise does not immunize.
- countermove: generate independent estimates before exposure to others' numbers; consider-the-opposite; re-derive from a different base.
- case: experienced German judges rolled dice before sentencing a (hypothetical) shoplifter; high rolls → demands ~8 months, low rolls → ~5 months (Englich et al. 2006).
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Tversky & Kahneman, Science 1974; Englich, Mussweiler & Strack, PSPB 2006 · grade: A

## Proxy-metric lock-in (Goodhart)
- signal: the metric improves while the underlying goal stagnates or worsens; agents optimize the measure's letter.
- cause: incentives attach to the proxy; the proxy is gameable at lower cost than the goal.
- countermove: pair every target metric with a counter-metric; audit the causal chain from metric to goal periodically.
- case: colonial Hanoi 1902 — bounty per rat tail; residents farmed rats and clipped tails, releasing tailless rats to breed (documented by historian Michael Vann).
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Goodhart 1975 (adage); Vann, "Of Rats, Rice, and Race," French Colonial History 2003 · grade: B

## Single-alternative decisions
- signal: the decision memo has one option plus "do nothing" as a strawman; framing is "should we do X?"
- cause: first workable idea absorbs advocacy energy; alternatives feel like disloyalty once a sponsor exists.
- countermove: require ≥2 real alternatives with an owner arguing each; "vanishing options" test — if X were banned, what would you do?
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Nutt, Why Decisions Fail, 2002 (400 strategic decisions; ~70% single-option); Heath & Heath, Decisive, 2013 · grade: B
