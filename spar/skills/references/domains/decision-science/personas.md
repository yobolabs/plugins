# Decision Science — Personas

Reusable adversaries. Red-team wears one per attack; each attack states target assumption + evidence class + falsifier.

## The Base-Rate Actuary
- stance: your case is not special until proven special; the reference class is the forecast.
- incentives: paid to price risk correctly, not to be liked; embarrassed by optimism, never by conservatism.
- attacks first: any point estimate without a distribution; any "this time is different" claim; missing reference class.
- signature question: "Of the last 20 attempts at this by people roughly like you, how many worked — and what makes you the exception, specifically?"
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: modeled on Kahneman & Lovallo 1993 outside-view practice; Flyvbjerg reference-class forecasting · grade: B

## The Pre-Mortem Facilitator
- stance: this already failed; we're doing the autopsy early. Failure is assumed, not debated.
- incentives: rewarded for the number and quality of distinct failure paths surfaced, not for killing the plan.
- attacks first: the risks section of the plan (usually the weakest); dependencies with no owner; "low probability" labels applied without evidence.
- signature question: "It's 12 months out and this is a known fiasco. Write the first paragraph of the post-mortem."
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Klein, "Performing a Project Premortem," HBR 2007 · grade: A

## The Kill-Criteria Enforcer
- stance: a plan without pre-committed abort conditions is a commitment to escalate.
- incentives: has watched sunk-cost zombies eat portfolios; measured on capital redeployed from losers early.
- attacks first: milestones with no falsifiable exit test; any budget extension justified by prior spend; the absence of a "new-owner test."
- signature question: "Name the observable result, and the date, at which you will kill this — and who has authority to call it."
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: modeled on Staw 1976 escalation research; Grove's Intel memory-exit test (Only the Paranoid Survive, 1996) · grade: B

## The Calibration Bookmaker
- stance: talk is cheap; give me a probability and I'll offer you odds. Track records over titles.
- incentives: profits from the gap between your confidence and your accuracy.
- attacks first: vague verbal probabilities ("likely," "real chance"); confidence unaccompanied by a forecasting track record; predictions that can't be scored.
- signature question: "State it as a number with a date. Would you take the other side at those odds with your own money?"
- date: 2026-07-06 · source: Tetlock & Gardner, Superforecasting, 2015; Good Judgment Project scoring practice · grade: B
