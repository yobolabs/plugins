# Decision Science — Brief

Distilled durable frames. Load before red-teaming any decision; each frame is an attack surface.

## Inside view vs outside view
Plans built from case-specifics (inside view) systematically underestimate cost/time/risk. Fix: start from the reference class — what happened to the last N similar attempts — then adjust. Reference-class forecasting is the only debiasing technique with regulatory adoption (UK Treasury Green Book optimism-bias uplifts, 2003; Danish transport ministry mandates).
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Kahneman & Lovallo, "Timid Choices and Bold Forecasts," Management Science 1993; Flyvbjerg, "From Nobel Prize to Project Management," PMJ 2006 · grade: A

## One-way vs two-way doors
Classify reversibility BEFORE deciding process weight. Type 2 (reversible) decisions should be made fast by small groups; Type 1 (irreversible) deserve slow deliberation. Most organizations run one-size-fits-all heavyweight process — or worse, treat one-way doors as casually as two-way ones.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Bezos, Amazon 2015 shareholder letter (primary) · grade: A

## Expected value vs ruin
Positive EV is not sufficient — a bet you cannot survive losing is a bad bet regardless of EV. Sequence matters (ergodicity): repeated exposure to small ruin probabilities compounds to near-certain ruin. Sizing discipline (Kelly criterion) caps exposure below the ruin threshold.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Kelly, Bell System Tech Journal 1956; Thorp, "The Kelly Criterion in Blackjack, Sports Betting, and the Stock Market," 2006 · grade: A

## Process vs outcome ("resulting")
Judging decisions by outcomes alone conflates decision quality with luck. A good process can lose; a bad process can win. Evaluate on information available at decision time. Empirically, decision PROCESS explains ~6x more variance in outcome quality than quality of analysis.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Baron & Hershey, "Outcome Bias in Decision Evaluation," JPSP 1988; Lovallo & Sibony, "The Case for Behavioral Strategy," McKinsey Quarterly 2010 (1,048 decisions) · grade: A

## Calibration beats conviction
Untrained experts' long-range forecasts barely beat chance (Tetlock: 82,361 forecasts, 284 experts). Trained, score-kept generalists (superforecasters) beat intelligence analysts with classified access by ~30%. Implication: demand probabilities + track records, not confidence displays.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Tetlock, Expert Political Judgment, 2005; Mellers et al., Psych Science 2014 (Good Judgment Project) · grade: A

## Pre-commitment: kill criteria + pre-mortem
Escalation of commitment is the default under sunk costs; the countermove is deciding abort conditions BEFORE evidence arrives. Pre-mortem ("it's 12 months later and this failed — why?") legitimizes dissent and surfaces risks brainstorming misses.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Staw, "Knee-Deep in the Big Muddy," OBHP 1976; Klein, "Performing a Project Premortem," HBR 2007 · grade: A

## Noise: independent judgments first
Group discussion before independent estimates destroys information — judgments correlate, confidence rises, accuracy doesn't. Collect written independent estimates BEFORE any discussion; aggregate mechanically.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Kahneman, Sibony & Sunstein, Noise, 2021 · grade: B

## Opportunity cost is the invisible line item
Every yes is a no to something. Decisions framed as "do X: yes/no?" fail ~50% of the time; framing as "X vs Y vs do-nothing" materially improves outcomes. ~70% of strategic decisions in Nutt's 400-decision sample considered only one alternative.
date: 2026-07-06 · source: Nutt, Why Decisions Fail, 2002 · grade: B
